Confidentiality

Confidentiality is an important part of psychological services. Information shared in therapy is kept private and is not disclosed to others without the client’s written consent, except in specific circumstances required by law or professional standards.

There are limits to confidentiality. Psychologists are legally and ethically required to take action if there is reason to believe that a client or another person may be at risk of serious harm. This includes situations involving risk of harm to oneself or others, concerns about the safety of a child or vulnerable person, or when records are required by a court of law.

When services involve children or adolescents, parents or guardians are typically involved in aspects of care. At the same time, efforts are made to respect the young person’s privacy and to create a safe and supportive therapeutic space.

Psychological records are maintained in accordance with the standards and ethical guidelines of the Nova Scotia Board of Examiners in Psychology.

If you have questions about confidentiality or privacy in psychological services, these can be discussed in more detail during the initial consultation.
