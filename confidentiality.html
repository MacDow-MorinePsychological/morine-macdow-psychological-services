Confidentiality

Confidentiality

Confidentiality is a central part of psychological services. Information shared in therapy is treated with care and respect for your privacy. Personal information is not shared with others without written consent, except in specific situations required by law or professional ethical standards.

Limits of Confidentiality

There are important limits to confidentiality. Psychologists are legally and ethically required to take action if there is reason to believe that a client or another person may be at risk of serious harm. This includes situations involving risk of harm to oneself or others, concerns about the safety of a child or vulnerable person, or when records are required by a court of law.

When services involve children or adolescents, parents or guardians are typically involved in aspects of care. At the same time, the young person’s privacy must also be respected to ensure a safe and supportive therapeutic space.

Professional Standards and Record Keeping

Psychological records are maintained in accordance with the professional standards governing the practice of psychology. This includes the requirements of the Nova Scotia Regulator of Psychology (formerly the Nova Scotia Board of Examiners in Psychology) and professional standards of the Canadian Code of Ethics for Psychologists.

Informed Consent

Before services begin, a detailed discussion of consent, confidentiality, privacy and record-keeping practices takes place. Clients and families are provided with consent forms and are encouraged to ask questions so they can make informed decisions about participating in psychological services.
